Understanding How AI Works

Before diving into the specific types of AI used in healthcare, let’s take a moment to break down how these AI models are actually created and put to use. It's a two-phase process: Model Development and Model Integration. Let’s walk through each step.

1. Model Development Phase:

  • Data Collection: The first step is gathering large amounts of data, such as medical records, imaging results, or patient interactions. This data is essential for teaching the AI model how to recognize patterns and make decisions.
  • Data Preprocessing: Once the data is collected, it needs to be cleaned and organized. This step ensures that the data is in a usable format and free from errors, making it suitable for training the AI model.
  • Model Selection: In this step, you choose the right type of AI model for the task. The choice of model affects how well the AI can solve the problem.
  • Training the Model: This is where the AI model learns. By feeding the preprocessed data into the model, the AI adjusts its internal settings (parameters) to reduce mistakes. The goal is to make the model accurate in its predictions.
  • Model Evaluation: After training, the model is tested on a different set of data that it hasn’t seen before (the validation dataset). This step ensures the model can make accurate predictions on new data, not just the data it was trained on.

2. Model Integration Phase:

  • System Integration: Once the model is trained and evaluated, it is integrated into a healthcare system or application. This allows the AI to start functioning in real-world environments.
  • Decision-Making: In this step, the AI model uses new, unseen data to make informed decisions or predictions. For example, it might assist in diagnosing a condition based on a new patient’s data.
  • Performance Monitoring: Even after integration, the AI model needs continuous monitoring. This ensures that the model remains accurate and reliable over time. If the model's performance drops, it may need to be updated or retrained with new data.

·         Ethics and Fairness: It’s crucial to ensure that our AI model operates ethically and fairly. This means avoiding biases in the data and ensuring that the AI’s decisions do not discriminate against any group. We must also be transparent about how the AI makes decisions and provide explanations when needed.

·         Regulations and Compliance: Finally, our AI model must comply with all relevant regulations and standards. This includes data privacy laws, medical device regulations, and industry standards. Ensuring compliance helps build trust and ensures the AI is used responsibly.

 

Main Subtypes of Machine Learning

Machine learning, a cornerstone of AI, can be categorized into three main types based on how models learn and the tasks they perform: Supervised Learning, Unsupervised Learning, and Reinforcement Learning.

Supervised Learning

Supervised learning models are trained on labeled data, where the input-output pairs are known. The goal is to predict outcomes based on new, unseen data.

Classification

In healthcare, doctors-in-training learn from patient case studies. Each case study includes patient symptoms, medical history, and the final diagnosis (“labeled data”). The trainee doctors study these case studies to recognize patterns and relationships between symptoms and diagnoses - the learning phase or training phase. So, when these now trained doctors encounter new patients with unfamiliar symptoms, they use their learned knowledge to diagnose the condition, along with their confidence level in the diagnosis.

Well, that is exactly, how Classification works: Classification is a type of supervised learning that predicts the category or class of a given data point based on input features.

How It Works:

·         Training Phase:

    • Labeled Data: The model is trained on a dataset where the input features (e.g., patient symptoms, medical history, lab results) are paired with known outcomes (e.g., disease diagnosis).
    • Learning Patterns: The model learns to recognize patterns and relationships between the input features and the corresponding classes.

·         Prediction Phase:

    • New Data: When presented with new, unseen patient data, the trained model uses the learned patterns to predict the most likely class or category.
    • Output: The model outputs a class label (e.g., “disease present” or “disease absent”), sometimes along with a confidence score indicating the likelihood of the prediction.

Common Algorithms: Decision Trees, Support Vector Machines (SVM), Boosting Algorithms, and Neural Networks

Example: Skin Lesion Classification Using Convolutional Neural Network With Novel Regularizer

Skin cancer is one of the most common types of cancer, and it's usually diagnosed by visually examining the skin, followed by more detailed tests like dermoscopic analysis, histopathological assessment, and a biopsy. However, because skin lesions can look very similar, it's challenging to classify them accurately using images alone.

To tackle this problem, researchers have turned to deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs), a type of artificial intelligence (AI) that excels at analyzing images. In this study, the authors introduce a new prediction model that uses a novel regularizer technique to classify skin lesions as either benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous). This model acts as a binary classifier, meaning it makes a clear distinction between the two types of lesions.

The results are impressive! The proposed model achieved an average accuracy of 97.49%, outperforming other state-of-the-art methods. The researchers tested the model's performance using the area under the curve (AUC) of the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve, which measures how well the model distinguishes between different types of lesions. The AUC scores for various comparisons were as follows:

·         Nevus vs. Melanoma: 0.77

·         Seborrheic Keratosis vs. Basal Cell Carcinoma: 0.93

·         Seborrheic Keratosis vs. Melanoma: 0.85

·         Solar Lentigo vs. Melanoma: 0.86

The model outperformed existing algorithms in 2019 and already had the potential to assist medical practitioners in accurately classifying various skin lesions, ultimately improving patient care.

 

Regression

Regression in supervised learning is like a healthcare professional estimating a patient’s recovery time based on their medical history and current health status. In Regression, the goal is to predict a continuous value based on input features. It's widely used in healthcare for predicting patient outcomes, estimating treatment effects, and forecasting healthcare costs.

  • How It Works:

o    Training Phase:

      • Labeled Data: The model is trained on a dataset where the input features (e.g., patient age, weight, blood pressure) are paired with known continuous outcomes (e.g., blood glucose levels, recovery time).
      • Learning Patterns: The model learns to recognize patterns and relationships between the input features and the continuous outcomes.

o    Prediction Phase:

      • New Data: When presented with new, unseen patient data, the trained model uses the learned patterns to predict the continuous outcome.
      • Output: The model outputs a continuous value (e.g., predicted blood glucose level, estimated recovery time).
  • Common Algorithms: Linear Regression, Polynomial Regression, Ridge Regression

Example: Estimation and Prediction of Hospitalization and Medical Care Costs Using Regression in Machine Learning

Medical costs are a significant recurring expense. Research indicates that factors like BMI, aging, and smoking contribute to higher personal healthcare costs. Estimating obesity-related healthcare expenses is essential for creating cost-effective prevention strategies.

 

Preventing obesity from a young age is a global health priority. To address study limitations, researchers use genetic variants as instrumental variables. By analyzing large public datasets, they predict the impact of BMI on healthcare expenses.

A multiview learning architecture leverages BMI information from diagnostic texts, IDs, and patient traits. A hierarchical perception structure identifies key words, health checks, and diagnoses for training data representations, as their significance varies in expense calculation.

This study from 2022 compares linear regression analysis, naive Bayes classifier, and random forest algorithms using a business analytics approach that combines statistical and machine-learning methods. Results show that linear regression has the highest accuracy (97.89%) in predicting healthcare costs, offering a predictive approach to financial statistics in healthcare.

 

Sidenote: Differences between Regression in Machine Learning and Regression in Statistics 

You might have learned about regression in your biostatistics courses and wondered how it compares to regression in machine learning.

In machine learning, regression is used to predict outcomes based on input data. For example, predicting whether a patient will be readmitted to the hospital based on their health metrics. Think of it as training a model to recognize patterns in patient data to forecast future outcomes. Machine learning can handle complex and non-linear relationships between variables, capturing intricate patterns that might not be obvious at first glance. The focus is on how well the model predicts outcomes, using metrics like accuracy and mean squared error. It’s all about ensuring the model’s predictions are reliable and accurate.

On the other hand, statistical regression is used to understand relationships between variables. For example, studying how a new medication affects blood pressure levels. Statistical regression involves testing hypotheses and drawing conclusions about the data. This often includes calculating p-values and confidence intervals to determine the significance of the results. Statistical regression typically focuses on simple, linear relationships, keeping the model straightforward and easy to interpret. The emphasis is on the significance of the relationships, using metrics like p-values and R-squared. It’s about understanding the strength and reliability of the connections between variables.

While the goals and methodologies might differ, the underlying principles of regression in both machine learning and statistics share many commonalities. Both aim to model relationships between variables. Whether predicting outcomes or understanding relationships, the core idea is to find how one or more variables influence another. Both use similar mathematical techniques. For example, linear regression in both fields involves fitting a line to data points to minimize the difference between observed and predicted values. Both use metrics like R-squared to assess the goodness of fit. This metric indicates how well the model explains the variability of the outcome variable. Both approaches often rely on certain assumptions. For example, linearity, independence of errors, and homoscedasticity (constant variance of errors) are common assumptions in both fields. Both require sufficient and relevant data to build accurate models. The quality and quantity of data are crucial for the reliability of the results.

In summary, machine learning regression is ideal for making predictions about patient outcomes and personalizing treatment plans, while statistical regression is perfect for understanding the effects of treatments and validating clinical hypotheses. Despite the differences in goals and methodologies, the underlying principles of regression in both machine learning and statistics share many commonalities.

Unsupervised Learning

The model is trained on unlabeled data, where the input-output pairs are not known. Instead, the model identifies patterns and structures within the data. Applications include clustering patients with similar symptoms for personalized treatment plans and dimensionality reduction for simplifying complex medical datasets.

Clustering

Imagine a medical research institute that has collected data from thousands of diabetic patients. The researchers have information such as blood sugar levels, insulin sensitivity, age, diet, and other health indicators, but they don’t know how these patients can be divided into specific subgroups. The goal is to discover natural subgroups within the diabetic population to better understand different profiles of the disease and tailor treatments accordingly.

How it works:

  1. Training Phase:
    • Unlabeled Data: The model is trained on a dataset where the input features (e.g., patient age, symptoms, lab results) are not paired with any specific labels or outcomes.
    • Finding Similarities: The model identifies patterns and similarities among the data points to group them into clusters.
  2. Clustering Phase:
    • New Data: When presented with new, unseen patient data, the trained model assigns the data points to the most appropriate clusters based on their features.
    • Output: The model outputs clusters of similar data points (e.g., groups of patients with similar symptoms or risk factors).

Common Algorithms: K-Means Clustering, Hierarchical Clustering, and DBSCAN.

Example: Clustering diabetic patients based on clinical characteristics can identify distinct profiles with varying levels of inflammation.

Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) is a complex condition influenced by various factors, with chronic high blood sugar and inflammation playing key roles in its development and progression. To provide more personalized care, it’s crucial to identify patient groups with increased inflammation.

In a study conducted at the Primary Health Care Center in Podgorica, Montenegro, 424 T2DM patients aged 30 to 85 were analyzed. By using hierarchical clustering, researchers grouped patients into four distinct clusters based on 12 clinical variables, including blood sugar levels and other metabolic indicators. Inflammation was measured using the neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR) and platelet-to-lymphocyte ratio (PLR).

  • Cluster 1: Moderate T2DM duration, good glycemic control, moderate inflammation.
  • Cluster 2: Shorter T2DM duration, better glycemic control than Cluster 1, mixed inflammation levels.
  • Cluster 3: Oldest patients, longest T2DM duration, poor glycemic control, highest inflammation.
  • Cluster 4: Youngest patients, best glycemic control, lowest coronary disease prevalence, mixed inflammation levels.

By identifying these clusters, healthcare providers can offer more personalized T2DM management, potentially reducing complications associated with the disease.

Dimensionality Reduction

Imagine an emergency care unit that deals with a large number of patients with diverse symptoms and medical histories. When patients arrive, they are assessed for a wide range of factors: vital signs (e.g., heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen levels), lab test results, medical history, current medications, and more. This creates a high-dimensional dataset, where each patient has numerous variables associated with their condition. However, not all of these variables are equally important for making quick and accurate decisions in an emergency.

In this scenario, the medical team uses a dimensionality reduction technique to simplify the data. By identifying the most critical variables—such as a combination of key vital signs, specific lab results, and relevant aspects of medical history—they reduce the number of variables from dozens to just a few that are most relevant for decision-making. This reduced dataset allows staff to focus on the most important information, making it easier and faster to assess the patient’s condition hence the degree of emergency.

That is basically, what dimensionality reduction is. It is a type of unsupervised machine learning where the goal is to reduce the number of input features while preserving as much information as possible. Dimensionality reduction is used to simplify complex datasets. This can also be used to visualize high-dimensional data.

How it works:

  1. Training Phase:

o    High-Dimensional Data: The model is trained on a dataset with many input features (e.g., patient age, weight, blood pressure, lab results).

o    Identifying Key Features: The model identifies the most important features and reduces the dataset to a lower-dimensional space while retaining the essential information.

  1. Reduction Phase:

o    New Data: When presented with new, unseen patient data, the trained model transforms the data into the reduced-dimensional space.

o    Output: The model outputs a simplified version of the data with fewer features, making it easier to analyze and interpret.

Common Algorithms: Principal Component Analysis (PCA), t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE), and Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA).

Example: Dimensionality Reduction using Principal Component Analysis for Cancer Detection based on Microarray Data Classification

Cancer remains one of the deadliest diseases globally. According to the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC), there were 14.1 million new cancer cases and 8.2 million cancer-related deaths in 2012. In recent years, DNA microarray technology has become a vital tool in analyzing and diagnosing cancer. This technology allows medical experts to examine gene expression data, which helps determine whether a person has cancer.

However, DNA microarray data is vast and complex, which can impact the accuracy of cancer classification. To address this, researchers have developed a classification scheme that includes dimension reduction. In this study, they used Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to reduce the data’s dimensions by calculating the variance proportion for eigenvector selection.

For the following classification process, two algorithms were chosen: Support Vector Machine (SVM) and Levenberg-Marquardt Backpropagation (LMBP). The results showed that the LMBP method was more stable, achieving an impressive average accuracy of 96.07%, compared to SVM’s 94.98%.

This study highlights the importance of using advanced machine learning techniques and dimension reduction methods to enhance the accuracy and stability of cancer classification. By leveraging these technologies, healthcare professionals can improve early cancer detection and treatment outcomes.

 

Reinforcement Learning 

Imagine a healthcare system managing patients with a chronic condition, such as diabetes. The goal is to optimize long-term treatment plans for each patient to improve their health outcomes over time. This is challenging because each patient responds differently to treatments, and their condition can fluctuate based on various factors such as diet, exercise, and stress levels.

In this scenario, a healthcare assistant (the agent) is responsible for helping manage the patient’s care. The healthcare assistant continuously monitors the patient’s health data, including blood sugar levels, medication adherence, and lifestyle habits (the states). Based on this information, the assistant makes decisions, such as adjusting medication dosage, recommending dietary changes, or scheduling follow-up appointments (the actions).

After each action, the healthcare assistant receives feedback (the reward) on how effective the action was. For example, if adjusting the medication dosage results in improved blood sugar control and fewer symptoms, the assistant receives a positive reward. Conversely, if the patient’s condition worsens, it receives a negative reward. The assistant uses this feedback to learn which actions are most effective for managing the patient’s condition.

This is how reinforcement learning works: The model learns by interacting with an environment and receiving feedback in the form of rewards or penalties. The goal is to maximize the cumulative reward over time.

Key Concepts in Reinforcement Learning:

  1. Agent: The decision-maker or learner in the reinforcement learning process. In our example, this would be the healthcare assistant.
  2. Actions: The set of possible moves or decisions the agent can make. Like adjusting medication dosage, scheduling follow-up appointments, or recommending lifestyle changes.
  3. Rewards: Feedback received by the agent after taking an action, indicating the success or failure of the action.
  4. States: The current situation or context in which the agent finds itself, represented by a set of features. In our example, this would be the patient’s current health status, including vital signs, lab results, and medical history.

How it works:

  1. Interaction Phase:
    • Exploration: The agent explores different actions to understand their effects on the environment.
    • Exploitation: The agent uses the knowledge gained from exploration to make decisions that maximize rewards.
  2. Learning Phase:
    • Policy: The agent develops a strategy or policy that defines the best action to take in each state to maximize cumulative rewards.
    • Value Function: The agent estimates the expected cumulative reward for each state-action pair.

Common Algorithms: Q-Learning, Deep Q-Networks (DQN), Policy Gradient Methods

Applications: Optimizing treatment strategies for chronic diseases and developing personalized rehabilitation programs based on patient progress.

Example: Encouraging Physical Activity in Patients with Diabetes: Intervention Using a Reinforcement Learning System

This study from 2016 explores how reinforcement learning (RL) can be used to encourage physical activity in patients with type 2 diabetes. Regular physical activity is crucial for managing diabetes, but many patients lead sedentary lifestyles. The researchers provided 27 sedentary type 2 diabetes patients with a smartphone-based pedometer and a personalized physical activity plan.

Patients received personalized messages through a reinforcement learning algorithm to encourage physical activity. These messages were sent between once a day and once a week. The RL algorithm was compared with a static policy for sending messages and weekly reminders.

Key Findings:

  • Participants who received messages from the RL algorithm increased their activity levels and walking pace.
  • The RL group experienced a greater reduction in blood glucose levels (HbA1c) compared to the control group.
  • The RL algorithm improved over time in predicting which messages would motivate participants to exercise.

Conclusion: Mobile phone apps combined with a reinforcement learning algorithm can significantly improve adherence to exercise in diabetic patients. This approach can be scaled to larger populations to enhance health and glycemic control. The study’s findings can also be applied to other areas where computer-led health coaching can positively impact human behavior.

A Closer Look on Deep Learning (DL)

DL is a subset of ML that utilizes neural networks with many layers (hence “deep”) to analyze various types of data. It is particularly effective in processing large amounts of unstructured data, such as medical images and patient records.

Basic Structure of Neural Networks

Input Layer:

  • Description: This is the first layer of the neural network where data enters the system. Each node in this layer represents a feature or piece of data from the patient’s profile, such as blood sugar levels, medication history, diet, and exercise patterns.
  • Analogy: The input layer is like gathering all the relevant information from the patient—symptoms, medical history, lifestyle habits, and recent test results. Just like how healthcare professionals need a comprehensive understanding of a patient's condition before making decisions, the input layer collects all the necessary data for the neural network to process.

Hidden Layers:

  • Description: These are the intermediate layers between the input and output layers. A neural network can have multiple hidden layers, each with several neurons that transform the input data through complex calculations. The hidden layers identify patterns and relationships within the data that aren't immediately obvious.
  • Analogy: Think of the hidden layers as the in-depth diagnostic processes that occur behind the scenes. This includes advanced tests and analysis, such as lab work, imaging, or consulting specialists. Just like these diagnostic steps help in understanding the root cause of the condition, the hidden layers help the network discern underlying patterns in the patient’s data.

Activation Functions:

·         Description: Think of activation functions as decision-making criteria. Activation functions in the hidden layers decide whether a neuron’s output should be passed to the next layer. They introduce non-linearity, enabling the network to understand complex patterns.

·         Analogy: Activation functions are like clinical judgments made after analyzing test results. They decide which findings are significant enough to be communicated or used for further diagnostics.

Output Layer:

·         Description: This is the final layer where the network produces the output, such as a treatment recommendation.

·         Analogy: The output layer is akin to the final diagnosis or treatment plan made by the healthcare team after processing all the data.

 

Understanding Learning in Neural Networks

Neural networks learn by processing data and adjusting their internal parameters to make better predictions over time. The learning process involves several key components: weights, activation functions, forward propagation, and backpropagation. Let’s explore how these work together.

  1. Weights:
    • Description: Weights are values assigned to the connections between neurons. They determine how much influence one neuron has on the next. When data is passed through the network, these weights scale the input, controlling how strong the signal is as it moves through the layers. The network adjusts these weights during learning to improve accuracy.
    • Analogy: Think of weights like the importance you assign to different symptoms when diagnosing a patient. If a symptom is more relevant, you give it more weight in your decision-making process. In the same way, the neural network uses weights to prioritize certain inputs over others.
  2. Forward Propagation:
    • Description: Forward propagation is the process by which input data moves through the network from the input layer to the output layer. At each layer, the data is processed by multiplying it by the weights, adding biases, and then applying activation functions. This step-by-step transformation ultimately leads to the final output (e.g., a prediction or classification).
    • Analogy: Forward propagation is like the entire diagnostic process in a medical setting. You start by gathering initial symptoms and test results (input), analyze them by weighing their importance and making judgments (using weights and activation functions), and finally reach a diagnosis or treatment recommendation (output). Each step builds on the previous one, refining the information until you reach a conclusion.
  3. Backpropagation:
    • Description: Backpropagation is the process that allows the network to learn from its mistakes. After forward propagation produces an output, the network compares this output to the actual result to calculate the error. Backpropagation then works backward through the network, adjusting the weights and biases to reduce this error. The goal is to improve the network’s predictions by minimizing the difference between predicted and actual results over time.
    • Analogy: Backpropagation is like reviewing your diagnostic process based on patient outcomes. After treating a patient, you assess how well the treatment worked (compare the network’s output with the actual result). If the outcome wasn’t ideal, you refine your approach for future diagnoses (adjust the network’s weights and biases) to improve accuracy. This continuous feedback loop helps both you and the network get better at making decisions over time.

In summary, in a neural network, learning occurs through the combined processes of forward propagation and backpropagation. During forward propagation, data is passed through the network, with weights adjusting the importance of each input and activation functions determining which signals move forward. This process continues layer by layer until the network produces an output.

Afterward, the network evaluates its output against the actual result. If there's an error, backpropagation kicks in, adjusting the weights and biases to reduce this error. This iterative process helps the network learn from its mistakes and make better predictions over time. Through this continuous cycle of prediction (forward propagation) and adjustment (backpropagation), the network gradually improves its ability to make accurate predictions.

Some Common Types of Neural Networks

Feedforward Neural Networks (FNN)

  • Description: This is the simplest type of neural network where information moves in one direction—from the input layer to the output layer without any cycles or loops.
  • Analogy: Imagine a straightforward medical examination where you gather patient information, analyze it, and make a diagnosis without revisiting any previous steps. This linear process is similar to how feedforward neural networks operate.

You might hear about Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs), which are a specific type of feedforward neural network. They consist of multiple layers of neurons, including at least one hidden layer between the input and output layers. MLPs are characterized by their use of non-linear activation functions, which allow them to model complex relationships between inputs and outputs.

An MLP is a type of FNN. The term “MLP” specifically refers to feedforward networks with multiple layers, including hidden layers. Thus, while all MLPs are FNNs, not all FNNs are MLPs. For example, a single-layer feedforward network without hidden layers is still an FNN but not an MLP.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

  • Description: CNNs are specialized neural networks designed to process and analyze visual data, making them ideal for medical imaging tasks. They can automatically detect features in images, such as edges, textures, and shapes, and use these features to make accurate predictions.

Key Components of CNNs:

  1. Convolutional Layers:
    • Description: These layers apply filters to the input image to create feature maps. Each filter focuses on a particular aspect of the image, helping to identify important details.
    • Analogy: Convolutional layers are like an interprofessional healthcare team examining a patient’s medical records. Each member of the team focuses on a different aspect of the patient’s health. For example, a cardiologist looks at heart-related data, a radiologist examines imaging results, a pathologist analyzes lab tests, and a nurse assesses the patient’s overall well-being and daily care needs. Together, they provide a comprehensive understanding of the patient’s condition by highlighting different important details.
  2. Pooling Layers:
    • Description: These layers reduce the dimensionality of the feature maps while retaining essential information. Pooling helps to reduce the amount of data while preserving the critical information needed for diagnosis.
    • Analogy: Pooling layers are like the interprofessional healthcare team summarizing their key findings to focus on the most important information. Instead of examining every detail, each team member highlights the most significant features. For example, the cardiologist might emphasize a critical heart condition, the radiologist points out a suspicious area in an imaging scan, the pathologist notes an abnormal lab result, and the nurse reports on the patient’s overall well-being and daily care needs. By pooling their insights, the team creates a concise and comprehensive report that focuses on the essential information needed for diagnosis and treatment.
  3. Fully Connected Layers:
    • Description: These layers combine features to make final predictions. They take the high-level features identified by the convolutional and pooling layers and use them to produce the final output.
    • Analogy: Fully connected layers are like synthesizing all the diagnostic information to make a final diagnosis or treatment recommendation. They bring together all the critical findings to provide a comprehensive conclusion.

 

Example: Automated Glioma Grading on Conventional MRI images Using Deep Convolutional Neural Networks

Gliomas are common brain tumors classified by the WHO into grades I-IV. Accurate grading is crucial for treatment and prognosis. This study from 2020 introduces two non-invasive methods to distinguish between low-grade (Grades II and III) and high-grade (Grade IV) gliomas using MRI images and deep learning, specifically CNNs.

Methods:

  1. Preprocessing: MRI images are prepared by aligning them correctly and fixing any inconsistencies in brightness.
  2. Two-Step Approach:
    • 3D Tumor Segmentation: Using a modified U-Net model, the tumor is identified and separated from the rest of the brain in 3D.
    • Tumor Classification:
      • Method 1: The slice with the largest tumor area is found, and a Mask R-CNN model is used to grade the tumor. To improve accuracy, the images are augmented (e.g., rotated, flipped) to create more training data.
      • Method 2: A 3D CNN (3DConvNet) is applied directly to the segmented tumor regions, using the full 3D context of the images for classification.

Results:

  • Performance:
    • 2D Mask R-CNN: Sensitivity (ability to correctly identify positive cases) 93.5%, Specificity (ability to correctly identify negative cases) 97.2%, Accuracy 96.3%.
    • 3DConvNet: Sensitivity 94.7%, Specificity 96.8%, Accuracy 97.1%.
  • Efficiency:
    • 3D Segmentation: Training takes ~10.5 hours, Testing takes ~50 seconds.
    • 2D Mask R-CNN: Training takes ~4 hours, Testing takes ~1 second.
    • 3DConvNet: Training takes ~2 hours, Testing takes ~0.25 seconds.

Conclusions: These automated methods remove the need for manual steps, helping doctors make treatment decisions and predict patient outcomes without needing surgical biopsies.

 

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)

Imagine you are a nurse monitoring a patient’s health over time, recording their vital signs and symptoms at regular intervals. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are designed to handle sequential data by maintaining a memory of previous inputs, making them well-suited for time-series analysis and patient monitoring. They can learn dependencies over time, which is crucial for understanding trends and predicting future events.

Key Components:

  1. Recurrent Layers:
    • Description: These layers maintain connections between neurons over time, allowing the network to retain information from previous inputs.
    • Analogy: Recurrent layers are like maintaining a patient’s health record over time, allowing you to see trends and patterns in their health. Each entry (e.g., daily vital signs) is connected to the previous entries, helping you understand how the patient’s health is changing over time and identify any trends or patterns.
  2. Problems with Recurrent Layers:
    • Description: Over time, it becomes challenging to remember all the details from the patient’s health record, especially the older entries.
    • Analogy: This is like struggling to remember important health events from the distant past because the details have faded over time.
    • Explanation: As the network processes more and more data over time, the gradients used to update the weights during training can become very small. This makes it difficult for the network to learn long-term dependencies, as the influence of older inputs diminishes. This is known as the vanishing gradient problem.
  3. Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Units:
    • Description: These are specialized RNN cells that address the vanishing gradient problem and improve long-term memory retention.
    • Analogy: LSTM units are like having a specialized memory system that helps you remember important health events and trends, even if they happened a long time ago. This ensures that significant health events (e.g., a previous surgery or a chronic condition) and trends are retained and can be recalled when needed, improving the accuracy of your health monitoring and decision-making.

Putting It All Together:

  • Recurrent Layers: Maintain a patient’s health record over time to see trends and patterns.
  • Problems with Recurrent Layers: Over time, it becomes challenging to remember all the details from the patient’s health record, especially the older entries (vanishing gradient problem).
  • LSTM Units: Use a specialized memory system to remember important health events and trends, even from the distant past, addressing the vanishing gradient problem.

Applications:

  • Time-Series Data: RNNs can analyze sequences of data points, such as ECG signals or patient vital signs, to detect patterns over time.
  • Patient Monitoring: RNNs can predict patient deterioration by analyzing continuous monitoring data.

Example: Application of a time-series deep learning model to predict cardiac dysrhythmias in electronic health records

Cardiac dysrhythmias (CD) are irregular heart rhythms affecting millions of Americans, leading to significant health issues and deaths. New strategies are urgently needed to tackle this growing problem.

This study from 2021 aims to predict cardiac dysrhythmias (CD) early by using electronic health record (EHR) data, allowing for timely diagnosis and treatment, ultimately improving cardiovascular outcomes. The Guideline Advantage (TGA) registry, which includes data from 70 clinics across the US, was used for this research.

Methods:

  1. Data Representation: Time-series cardiovascular health (CVH) measures and their collection times were transformed into numerical vectors.
  2. Model Selection: A deep learning technique called long-short term memory (LSTM) was used to predict CD from these vectors. The performance of this model was compared with other models like deep neural networks, logistic regression, random forest, and Naïve Bayes.

Results:

  • The LSTM model outperformed other models, achieving the highest prediction performance with an average area under the receiver operator curve (AUROC) of 0.76.
  • Other models’ AUROC scores were: 0.71 for deep neural networks, 0.66 for logistic regression, 0.67 for random forest, and 0.59 for Naïve Bayes.
  • Blood pressure was identified as the most influential feature in predicting CD.

These findings suggest that using EHR data and advanced deep learning models like LSTM can help prevent CD in outpatient settings by improving surveillance and management of CVH.

 

There is more to neural networks, as they form the main algorithm of generative AI (Chapter 3). Additionally, it is important to mention that there are various types of neural networks, each designed for specific tasks. For example, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are ideal for working with graph-structured data, Capsule Networks (CapsNets) preserve spatial relationships between features, and Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) feature learnable activation functions on edges instead of fixed activation functions on nodes. This unique structure allows KANs to achieve better accuracy and interpretability compared to traditional Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs). These advanced neural networks expand the capabilities of AI, enabling more sophisticated and specialized applications. Notably, KANs are an example of neural networks that do not rely on traditional weight matrices.
