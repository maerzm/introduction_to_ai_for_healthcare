Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, Deep Learning — and how they relate

Imagine you're exploring the world of healthcare technology, where terms like Artificial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning (ML), and Deep Learning (DL) pop up frequently. It can be overwhelming, but let's simplify these concepts a bit.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) — The Big Picture

Think of AI as the umbrella term that covers everything related to making machines "smart". When we talk about AI, we’re referring to any system or technology that can perform tasks that typically require intelligence—like understanding language, making decisions, or recognizing patterns. In healthcare, AI can be seen in systems that help diagnose diseases, manage patient data, or even interact with patients through virtual assistants.

But AI is a broad field, and under this umbrella, there are more specialized areas. In the analogy of a hospital, AI would be like the whole hospital.

Machine Learning (ML) — subset of AI:
Machine Learning is a specialized department within the AI hospital. ML is the part of AI where computers learn from data to make predictions or decisions. Rather than being explicitly programmed, these systems find patterns in data. In healthcare, ML is used to predict patient outcomes, detect diseases early, and personalize treatment plans.

Deep Learning (DL) — subset of ML:
Deep Learning is a highly specialized unit within the ML department. DL uses complex neural networks to process vast amounts of data and uncover intricate patterns. The key feature of DL is its ability to automatically extract important details from raw data with minimal human intervention.

Narrow AI, AI Agents, and General AI

Imagine you're a health sciences student stepping into a futuristic hospital. As you walk through the halls, you see a world filled with technology helping doctors and nurses every step of the way. From virtual assistants guiding patients to their appointments to advanced software analyzing medical images, it’s clear that artificial intelligence is everywhere. But there's a secret about these AI systems that you, as a student of health sciences, should know: they’re not as smart as they might seem.

These AI systems are what we call Narrow AI or Weak AI. They’re like skilled specialists—excellent at specific tasks but incapable of doing anything beyond what they’re programmed for. For example, imagine Siri or Alexa, those voice assistants you use to set reminders or play music. They’re great at following your commands but ask them to explain the latest medical breakthrough or perform surgery, and you’ll quickly see their limits. Similarly, the recommendation engines on Netflix or Amazon seem to know your tastes perfectly, but they can’t hold a conversation or understand why you like what you like.

In the healthcare setting, Narrow AI has made incredible strides. Picture a virtual assistant that helps patients manage their chronic diseases. It can remind them to take medication, provide advice on managing symptoms, and even schedule appointments. However, it doesn’t truly "understand" health or medicine—it’s simply following a complex set of rules and data patterns. Then there are AI-powered diagnostic tools, which can detect diseases from medical images with remarkable accuracy. These tools are incredibly helpful, but they’re not "thinking" in the way humans do. They’re processing data—millions of images, patterns, and statistics—to make predictions.

How do AI Agents fit in? These are a bit more advanced than traditional Narrow AI. AI Agents can perform a range of tasks autonomously and can even learn from their environment to improve their performance over time. Imagine an AI Agent in a hospital setting that not only schedules appointments but also monitors patient vitals, adjusts medication dosages based on real-time data, and alerts healthcare providers to potential issues before they become critical. These agents can operate with a degree of autonomy and adaptability that traditional Narrow AI cannot.

However, even AI Agents have their limitations. They are still bound by their programming and the data they have been trained on. They don’t possess true understanding or consciousness. They can adapt and learn within their specific domain, but they cannot transfer this learning to entirely new and unrelated tasks.

But what about General AI or Strong AI? This is where the story takes a turn into the realm of science fiction and dreams of the future. General AI would be like a doctor who not only diagnoses and treats patients but also learns, adapts, and understands complex situations with human-like intuition and creativity. Imagine an AI that could switch seamlessly between discussing a patient’s treatment plan, debating ethical dilemmas with doctors, and even comforting a patient who’s feeling anxious. This kind of AI would be capable of anything a human could do, and perhaps even more.

However, this level of AI doesn’t exist—at least, not yet. Scientists and engineers are working on it, but for now, General AI remains a concept more at home in science fiction novels than in actual hospitals.

In summary, today’s AI systems are powerful tools, but they’re just that—tools. They assist, analyze, and support, but they don’t think, feel, or understand. The dream of an AI that can truly match human intelligence is still a vision for the future. Until then, it’s up to healthcare professionals like you to provide the understanding, empathy, and critical thinking that AI cannot.

Discriminative models vs generative models

In healthcare, we often need models that can help us make decisions based on patient data. Discriminative models focus on understanding the relationship between specific patient features (such as age, blood pressure, or cholesterol levels) and a target outcome (like the presence of a disease). These models essentially draw a line that separates different classes, helping to classify patients based on their data. Mathematically, discriminative models estimate the conditional probability P(y∣X) which is the probability of a particular outcome y (such as diabetes) given the patient's features X (like blood sugar levels, BMI, and age).

For instance, if a patient tests positive for a specific biomarker, healthcare providers use these models to evaluate the likelihood that the patient has a particular disease. Similarly, if a patient has a family history of breast cancer along with a certain genetic mutation, discriminative models help assess the risk of developing breast cancer by considering these factors.

Examples of discriminative models include:

·         Linear Regression: Predicts continuous outcomes, such as blood pressure levels.

·         Logistic Regression: Handles binary classifications, like determining if a patient has a disease or not.

·         Decision Trees: Classifies or predicts outcomes by splitting data into branches.

·         Random Forest: Combines multiple decision trees to improve prediction accuracy.

·         Support Vector Machines (SVM): Finds the optimal boundary that separates different patient groups.

·         K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN): Classifies or predicts outcomes, e.g. by comparing patients to their closest neighbors in the data.

·         Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM): Builds models sequentially to minimize prediction errors.

·         Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs): Analyzes medical images, like MRI scans, to detect abnormalities.

·         Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs): Tracks patient data over time, useful for monitoring disease progression.

Chapter 2 focuses primarily on these discriminative models, which play a critical role in healthcare decision-making.

On the other hand, generative models try to understand how the data is created. Mathematically, generative models estimate the joint probability P(X,y), which represents the probability of both the patient's features X (like smoking habits, age, and cholesterol levels) and the outcome y (such as heart disease) occurring together. For instance, a generative model might determine the likelihood of a patient being a smoker, having high cholesterol, and developing heart disease all at once. From this joint probability, generative models can also derive the conditional probability P(y∣X), like generartive models, but they go a step further by generating new examples of data.

This allows the model not only to predict outcomes but also to generate new data based on the relationships it has learned.

For example, if we know the joint probability of a patient having certain lifestyle factors (such as smoking and obesity) and high cholesterol, generative models can help assess the overall risk of developing heart disease. Moreover, these models can simulate how diseases might manifest under different conditions, aiding in the creation of patient risk profiles.

Examples of generative models include:

·         Naive Bayes: Often used to diagnose diseases by modeling the joint probability of features and conditions.

·         Hidden Markov Models (HMM): Tracks sequences, such as the progression of a disease over time.

·         Restricted Boltzmann Machines (RBMs): Used for collaborative filtering and patient recommendations.

·         Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs): Creates synthetic medical images to enhance training datasets.

·         Variational Autoencoders (VAEs): Generates and reconstructs medical data for improved analysis.

·         Transformer Models: Generates sequences of medical text or images, useful in predictive modeling.

·         Diffusion Models: Enhances medical imaging by generating high-quality images.

Chapter 3 dives deeper into these generative models, exploring how they contribute to advancing healthcare technology.

 

A Brief History of AI

Early Foundations

The idea of intelligent machines dates back to ancient mythology and early philosophy, where thinkers imagined mechanical beings that could mimic human actions. However, AI as we know it began to take shape in the mid-20th century.

1950s-1960s: The Birth of AI

The 1950s marked the formal beginning of AI. In 1950, Alan Turing proposed the Turing Test, a concept that sought to measure a machine’s ability to exhibit intelligent behavior indistinguishable from that of a human. Then, in 1956, John McCarthy officially coined the term “Artificial Intelligence” during the Dartmouth Conference, which is often seen as the birth of AI as an academic field. The 1960s saw the development of early AI programs, like ELIZA, a chatbot that mimicked human conversation, and Shakey the Robot, one of the first robots to navigate its environment autonomously.

1970s-1980s: AI Winters and Early Successes

The enthusiasm for AI hit a roadblock during the First AI Winter (1974-1980). This period of stagnation was triggered by the Lighthill Report in 1973, which criticized AI's slow progress, leading to funding cuts. Failures in machine translation and early neural networks, known as perceptrons, further dampened the optimism. Many AI projects were abandoned, and the field struggled to attract resources.

However, the 1980s brought a resurgence of interest with the emergence of machine learning algorithms and expert systems, which could mimic the decision-making abilities of human experts. Yet, this momentum was short-lived, as the Second AI Winter (1987-1993) struck due to the collapse of the LISP machine market and the unreliability of expert systems. Once again, AI faced waning interest and reduced activities.

1990s-2000s: Transitional Period and Renewed Interest

The 1990s and early 2000s are sometimes referred to as a third AI Winter, caused by disappointment with Japan's ambitious Fifth Generation Computer Systems project and the high costs of AI research. Despite this, foundational work in machine learning and neural networks during this time laid the groundwork for future breakthroughs.

AI saw renewed interest as advances in computational power and data availability spurred progress. A pivotal moment came in 1997 when IBM's Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov, demonstrating AI’s growing capabilities.

2010s-Present: Rapid Advancements

The 2010s witnessed significant progress in machine learning and deep learning. In 2011, IBM Watson made headlines by winning “Jeopardy!”, showcasing AI's ability to process natural language and retrieve vast amounts of information. This achievement brought AI into the healthcare spotlight, leading to applications in areas like oncology for treatment recommendations.

In 2012, AlexNet won the ImageNet competition, demonstrating the power of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) in image recognition. This breakthrough had direct implications for medical imaging, enabling more accurate AI-based diagnostic tools.

In 2014, Google’s DeepMind developed AlphaGo, which made history by defeating a human Go champion. The techniques behind AlphaGo were later applied to healthcare, particularly in predicting patient deterioration and diagnosing eye diseases through retinal scans.

In 2017, the publication of "Attention Is All You Need" by Vaswani et al. marked a cornerstone in the development of natural language processing (NLP) models. This influential paper introduced the transformer architecture, which replaced older sequential models like RNNs and LSTMs, leading to more efficient and powerful processing of text data. Despite its foundational role, this seminal work remains a preprint.

Building on the transformer architecture, 2018 saw the introduction of BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) by Google AI. BERT revolutionized NLP by enabling a more nuanced understanding of context in text.

In 2022, ChatGPT by OpenAI made waves with its ability to generate human-like text, revolutionizing NLP. ChatGPT has found applications in virtual health assistants, providing medical advice and support. BERT can be considered a foundational component in the evolution of models like GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer), including ChatGPT. While BERT and GPT are distinct in their architectures and purposes, BERT's innovations influenced the development of models like GPT-3, which underpins ChatGPT.

By 2023, AI had made significant strides in drug discovery, with AI models predicting potential drug candidates and accelerating the development of new treatments for diseases like cancer and Alzheimer’s.

In 2024, the development of multimodal AI models, such as OpenAI's GPT-4 and Google DeepMind's Gemini, opened new possibilities in healthcare. These models, capable of processing text, images, and audio, are being used to analyze complex medical data, improving diagnostic accuracy and personalized treatment plans.
